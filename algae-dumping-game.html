The Two Dumping Tests (WTO Rules)

Test 1: Is the export price below the domestic price?   P < P*?

Test 2: Is the export price below the average cost of production?   P < AC?

Only one test needs to be satisfied for a dumping finding. The dumping margin = P* − P (using Test 1).

Export price P = $15 Domestic price P* = $30 AC (with exports) = $800 + $10×60) / 60 = $23.33

🎓 Key Takeaways

1. Why dumping happens: Fixed costs create a wedge between AC and MC. Once FC is covered by domestic sales, exporting at any price above MC adds profit — even if that price is far below AC. Economica exports at $15 because $15 > $10 = MC.

2. Two tests, one finding: Under WTO rules, dumping is found if P < P* (domestic price) or P < AC. Both conditions are satisfied here ($15 < $30 and $15 < $23.33), so the antidumping duty is legally justified.

3. The antidumping paradox: Cocoland consumers were benefiting from cheap AFC imports at $15. The antidumping duty eliminated those imports entirely — protecting Cocoland producers but harming Cocoland consumers. Antidumping policy is not always welfare-improving for the importing country as a whole.

4. Strategic use of antidumping: Because the WTO allows antidumping duties whenever the two tests are met, domestic firms often use these complaints as a protectionist tool — even when the foreign firm's pricing behavior is economically rational. This is why antidumping provisions are considered one of the most overused instruments in trade policy.
