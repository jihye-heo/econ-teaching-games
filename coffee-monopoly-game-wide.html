So the cooperative is forced to act like a price-taker, setting P = PW = MC. Free trade eliminates monopoly power!

ACT 4
Equivalent Quota — The Monopoly Awakens Same import volume M₂=120 — but now the cooperative regains market power
⚠ The Critical Difference

Under a Quota, Imports Run Out

Under the tariff, imports are always available at $25 — keeping the cooperative in check. Under a quota of 120 bags, once those 120 bags are sold, no more imports can enter. The cooperative is now the only remaining seller. Its market power returns. It faces the residual demand D − M₂ and can optimize like a monopolist again.

What We Know

The government sets a quota M₂ = 120 bags — the same import volume as under the tariff. Total demand is still QD = 300 − 4P. Once the 120 imported bags are sold, the cooperative is the only seller left.

Conceptual Check
Why does the quota allow the cooperative to charge a higher price than the tariff, even though both restrict imports to the same 120 bags?

🎓 Key Takeaways

1. Free trade eliminates monopoly power: The horizontal export supply at PW disciplines the monopolist — it must price at marginal cost.

2. Tariff = Competitive outcome: Even with a monopolist, a tariff produces the same welfare result as under perfect competition. The cooperative still faces import competition at every quantity.

3. Quota ≠ Tariff with a monopolist: The quota shelters the cooperative — once imports run out, it regains market power and charges $30 instead of $25. Same 120 bags imported, but $400 more welfare lost.

4. Why the WTO pushes tariffication: Replacing quotas with equivalent tariffs eliminates this monopoly shelter effect. This is one major reason the WTO has encouraged countries to convert quotas to tariffs — even holding import quantities fixed, the welfare gain from switching to a tariff can be large.
